# CTA Design Principles

How to think about CTAs that actually convert.

## Core Truth

A CTA's job is to make the next step obvious and compelling. The best CTA depends entirely on what you're asking someone to do and where they are in their journey.

---

## Principle 1: Match CTA Urgency to Business Context

- **Emergency services**: Phone number IS the CTA. Huge, high-contrast, tappable.
- **Professional services**: Lower commitment first. "Book a consultation" or "Get a free assessment."
- **E-commerce**: "Add to cart" with clear pricing visible.
- **Creative/agency**: Relationship-building. "View our work", "Let's talk."
- **SaaS**: Free trial > demo > contact sales. Don't ask for credit card upfront.

**The Pattern**: Ask for the smallest meaningful commitment that moves the relationship forward.

---

## Principle 2: Placement — After Value, Not Before

### The Golden Rule: Make Your Case First

**Good sequence**: Problem statement -> Solution -> Proof -> **Now ask for action**

### Placement Sweet Spots

1. **After hero** — for people already convinced
2. **Mid-page, after explaining the problem** — "Ready to fix this?"
3. **After social proof** — testimonials built trust, now convert
4. **Pre-footer banner** — last chance before they leave (highest converting)
5. **Sticky on mobile** — only for high-urgency services, use sparingly

### How Many CTAs Per Page?

1-2 per screenful maximum. Low urgency: 1-2 total. High urgency (landing pages): 3-4 max.

---

## Principle 3: Copy — Words Matter More Than Design

### Specific Beats Generic

| Generic (Weak) | Specific (Strong) |
|----------------|-------------------|
| Contact Us | Call for Same-Day Service |
| Learn More | See How We Cut Energy Bills 30% |
| Get Started | Book Your Free Roof Inspection |
| Submit | Get Your Custom Quote |

**The test**: If your CTA could work on any business's website, it's too generic.

### Action Verbs

The verb should describe the immediate next step, not the end goal:
- "Book a consultation" (not "Get better finances")
- "Download the checklist" (not "Improve your marketing")

---

## Principle 4: Visual Design

- **Squint test**: Can you still see the CTA? If not, not enough contrast.
- **One primary CTA per section**: Solid fill, high contrast, largest size.
- **Secondary CTA**: Outline or ghost style, smaller.
- **Whitespace**: Give CTAs generous breathing room above and below.

---

## Principle 5: Context-Specific CTAs

- **Homepage**: Main action for entire business + secondary path
- **Service pages**: Service-specific action ("Book a roof inspection"), NOT generic "Contact us"
- **About page**: Softer CTA — "Work with us", "See our projects"
- **Pricing page**: Per-tier CTA + "Not sure? Talk to us"
- **Contact page**: The form IS the CTA. Minimal other CTAs.

---

## Anti-Patterns

1. "Learn More" that goes nowhere — be specific
2. Giant banner with generic motivational copy
3. Multiple competing CTAs of equal weight
4. CTAs before value has been established
5. Friction-heavy CTAs — "Download our comprehensive enterprise-grade whitepaper"
6. Buried CTA in paragraph text — use visually distinct button

---

## Mobile Considerations

- Minimum touch target: 44px tall
- Full-width buttons on mobile for hero/banner CTAs
- Stack buttons vertically: primary on top, secondary below
- Phone number CTAs: `<a href="tel:...">` links, large and obvious
- Sticky CTAs: only for emergency/high-intent pages
